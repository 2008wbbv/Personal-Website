Why People Should Read More and Why it is an Undervalued Skill in Society

Back to Main Blog

Introduction

In today's fast-paced world, reading is often seen as a luxury or a waste of time. However, reading is actually a valuable skill that can provide many benefits to individuals and society as a whole. In this blog post, we will explore why people should read more and why it is an undervalued skill in society.

Benefits of Reading

  • Improves Cognitive Function: Reading can improve cognitive function, including memory, concentration, and critical thinking skills.
  • Expands Knowledge and Vocabulary: Reading exposes individuals to new ideas and information, expanding their knowledge and vocabulary.
  • Enhances Empathy and Understanding: Reading can enhance empathy and understanding by exposing individuals to different cultures, perspectives, and experiences.
  • Reduces Stress: Reading can reduce stress by providing a relaxing and enjoyable activity that can distract individuals from their problems.
  • Increases Creativity: Reading can increase creativity by exposing individuals to new ideas and perspectives that can inspire them to create new things.

Undervalued Skill in Society

Despite the many benefits of reading, it is often undervalued in society. Reading is seen as a leisure activity rather than a valuable skill, and individuals who read regularly may be viewed as lazy or unproductive. This is unfortunate, as reading can provide many benefits to individuals and society as a whole.

How to Encourage More Reading

  • Start Young: Encourage children to read by reading to them regularly and providing them with age-appropriate books.
  • Make Reading a Habit: Set aside time each day to read, even if it is only for a few minutes.
  • Join a Book Club: Joining a book club can provide motivation to read and can also provide a social aspect to reading.
  • Make Reading Accessible: Make reading materials easily accessible by having books and other reading materials readily available in the home or workplace.
